Fundraising Narrative — Draft 2

I — The Problem

Every robot on Earth is blind.

Not literally. They have cameras, force sensors, depth scanners, tactile arrays. They can see objects. They can measure distance. They can detect contact.

But they don't understand tasks.

Put the most advanced robot in the world in front of a dishwasher it hasn't been specifically trained on, and it is lost. Not because it can't see the dishes. Because it has no concept of what "loading a dishwasher" actually is — where the critical decision points are, what separates a competent grasp from a catastrophic one, why the spray arm matters, when to slow down, when to commit.

That knowledge — the deep structure of how tasks work — does not exist anywhere in the robotics stack. Not in the foundation models. Not in the training data. Not in the simulation environments. Not in any product you can buy.

It is the single most important missing piece in physical AI. And we built it.

II — The Market

$8 billion deployed. Same problem everywhere.

$8 billion has been deployed into robot foundation models in the last eighteen months. Physical Intelligence. Figure. Covariant. Generalist. Google DeepMind. Tesla. Nvidia pouring billions into simulation infrastructure.

All of them are chasing the same loop: collect data, train a policy, deploy, repeat.

And all of them have the same problem.

The task knowledge gets entombed inside the policy weights. It is not portable. It is not interpretable. It is not reusable. Pi cannot use what Figure learned. Figure cannot use what Tesla learned. Every new task, every new embodiment, every new environment — you start over. You retrain from scratch with millions of dollars in compute and months of data collection.

Meanwhile, the planet generates more useful data every single day than these labs could collect in a decade. Factory workers. Warehouse operators. Surgeons. Cooks. Mechanics. Cleaners. Every human performing a physical task is demonstrating exactly the knowledge these models need.

But nobody can use it. Not because the data doesn't exist. Because there is no system in the world that can extract what it means.

You can label it. You can segment it. You can annotate every frame. You still don't know how a task works. You know what happened in a video. Those are completely different things.

The bottleneck in robotics is not data volume. It is not compute. It is not model size. The bottleneck is that nobody has built the interpretation layer.

III — The Insight

We found the answer in the body.

We didn't find the answer in AI. We found it in developmental biology.

When a salamander loses a limb, the cells at the wound site don't contain a blueprint for "leg." There is no instruction manual. No master plan stored in DNA. Instead, there is an energy field — a morphogenetic field — that encodes the target form. Every cell reads the field and navigates toward the correct structure on its own. The field doesn't tell each cell what to do. It tells every cell where to go.

Change the cells — the field still works. Transplant tissue from a different part of the body — it reads the field and becomes the right thing. The intelligence is not in the cells. The intelligence is in the field.

This is Michael Levin's work at Tufts. It is one of the most important scientific discoveries of the last twenty years. And it is mathematically identical to what we build for robots.

We construct an energy field for physical tasks.

Not a policy that tells a specific robot what to do step by step — that breaks the moment anything changes. An energy field that encodes what the task looks like when it's done right. Any robot reads the field and finds its own way there.

Change the robot — the field still works. Change the environment — the field still works. Change the model architecture — the field still works. Because the field doesn't encode how a particular body moves. It encodes how reality is structured when a task succeeds.

IV — The Architecture

The ontological layer for physical AI.

For every task, we learn two things from diverse human demonstrations:

A task manifold. The topology of the task itself — a low-dimensional map of every meaningful state and transition, independent of any body. Picking up the dish. Choosing the rack position. Placing. Confirming clearance. This is the skeletal structure of competence. The ontology of the task — what states exist and how they relate.

An energy landscape over that manifold. A scalar field that scores every configuration. Low energy: goal states, stable success, deep basins. High energy: failure boundaries, ridges, danger zones. The gradient points downhill toward success. Saddle points mark where decisions must be made. Bifurcations mark where small errors cascade into failures.

The manifold defines what exists. The landscape defines what's optimal. The gradient says how to get there.

This is not a metaphor for what Palantir built. It is the same architecture.

Palantir took messy, siloed intelligence data — from the CIA, NSA, financial institutions — and built an ontological layer: a system that defines entities, relationships, and patterns across domains, making raw data queryable and actionable regardless of where it came from. The data sources change. The ontological layer remains. It became the most valuable part of the stack. Today Palantir is worth $260 billion.

We are doing the same thing for the physical world.

Our manifold is the ontology — it defines the entities and states of physical tasks. Our energy landscape is the scoring layer — it defines which states are optimal and how to navigate between them. The robots change. The model architectures change. The training paradigms change. The landscape remains. Because task physics don't care how you train.

V — The Product

330 megabytes. Not data. The map.

Here is what hits a robot company's servers.

A 330-megabyte frozen package. Not our data. Not our demonstrations. Not labeled video. The map.

They run 50 of their own robot episodes through a localization adapter. Takes hours. And then their robot knows where it is on our landscape for that task — and more importantly, it knows which direction leads to success.

For training: the landscape replaces the brittle, hand-crafted sparse rewards that are the single biggest bottleneck in robot RL today. Instead of "did you succeed? yes/no" — the robot gets a dense signal at every timestep. Not just whether it's failing, but where it's failing and why. "Policy fails at spray arm clearance in 29% of episodes because it rushes through rack positioning." No other system in the world can produce that sentence.

For evaluation: subgoal-level scoring, coverage analysis, failure diagnosis by task phase. Every lab we talk to says this is the eval system they desperately need. Nobody has built it because you cannot build it without the ontological structure underneath.

For control: the gradient of the energy landscape injects directly into action generation at inference time. No retraining. The policy says what's physically possible for this body. The landscape says where the task wants to go. You blend them. This is classifier guidance for robotics — and we already have the integration designed for pi0.6's flow-matching architecture.

Three product surfaces from one underlying system. Each independently valuable. Together, they make the landscape load-bearing infrastructure for anyone building robots.

VI — The Data Engine

The chaos is the signal.

The landscape is only as good as the data that builds it. And here is where our moat becomes a wall.

What makes the energy landscape work is not volume of data. It is variance. An expert loading a dishwasher in a restaurant kitchen in Bangalore. A college student fumbling through it in an apartment in San Francisco. A factory worker in Shenzhen. A grandmother in Lagos. The expert flows downhill through the landscape — smooth, low energy, efficient. The novice wanders along ridges — high energy, hesitation, recovery. The difference between those trajectories is where task intelligence lives.

You could spend a billion dollars collecting perfect demonstrations in a lab. It would produce a worse landscape than what we build from the chaos of the real world. Because the chaos is the signal.

So we built the collection engine.

Our app lets anyone on Earth record themselves performing a task and get paid. We assess quality and relevance automatically. If it's useful, it enters the pipeline. If not, it doesn't. The contributor gets paid either way for their time. The economics work because we don't need perfection. We need diversity at scale.

And then the flywheel turns.

More demonstrations. Richer manifold. Better reward signals. More labs want access. More revenue. We subsidize more collection. More diversity. Deeper landscape. The compounding advantage accelerates every cycle.

We chose the Tesla model, not the Waymo model. Waymo spent billions deploying proprietary vehicles to map the world. Slow, expensive, and brittle. Tesla put sensors on millions of cars that people already drove. Every driver generates data. Every mile makes the model smarter. Zero marginal cost.

Our app is the Tesla fleet. The humans are the drivers. The landscape is FSD. And every task that every person performs anywhere in the world makes it better.

VII — The Identity

What this company is.

We are not a data broker. We never sell raw data. The difference between us and Scale AI is the difference between crude oil and jet fuel. Our customers receive distilled task intelligence — the manifold, the landscape, the gradient — in a package they can plug into any training pipeline.

We are not an eval company. Eval is one surface of the product, not the product itself. The landscape is simultaneously reward model, eval system, diagnostic engine, and control signal.

We are not a foundation model company. We are not competing with Pi or DeepMind on parameters. We are building the layer they need and cannot build themselves.

We are building the task intelligence layer for physical AI.

The interpretation system that sits between the world's data and the world's robots and makes one legible to the other. The ontological layer for the physical world.

And along the way — because we will have the deepest understanding of task physics of anyone on the planet — we build our own models. Not the biggest. The smartest. Compact, efficient models that read the landscape and execute. We don't need a billion parameters. We need the map.

VIII — The Proof

Not speculative. Grounded.

The research is not speculative. The theoretical foundation is published. The morphogenetic framework is grounded in Levin's bioelectric morphogenesis and recent mathematical proofs that representational geometry in neural networks is a direct consequence of symmetry in data statistics. The manifold's shape is analytically predictable from task structure. It is not a black box.

Our research lead developed this framework independently from years of experience in robot learning at Tesla. He is co-authoring the white paper. He speaks to our team multiple times a week. This is his primary research bet.

The execution plan is phased and concrete. Baseline established on standard benchmarks. Landscape model trained on contact-rich datasets with force-torque and tactile sensing. Validated on a golden set of curated demonstrations. Integrated with pi0.6's advantage conditioning. Cross-embodiment transfer proven from human video to robot guidance.

We have strategic engagements with DeepMind, NVIDIA, and several leading robotics labs. The app is live. The data pipeline works. The slope of data ingestion is steeper than anyone else in the space.

IX — The Ask

$20 million. The window is now.

We are raising $20 million.

The capital buys three things: the data moat, the research, and the first shipped product.

First — subsidize the marketplace until the flywheel is self-sustaining. Contributors get paid. That is our primary variable cost and it is what produces the diversity that no one else has.

Second — the research team to build the landscape across task domains, validate it against state-of-the-art policies, and publish the results.

Third — ship the first landscape packages to partners and prove that our reward signal measurably improves downstream robot performance on real tasks.

The window is now. Foundation models are commoditizing. Open-source is catching up. Every major lab is starting to hire commercial teams because the model alone isn't the business. The question the entire industry is about to confront is: what layer actually captures the value?

A thousand flowers are blooming in robotics right now. Flow-matching. JEPA. World models. Simulation. Reinforcement learning. Every one of them is getting siloed. Every one of them needs the same thing. None of them are building it.

We are.

Abundance turns human behavior into task intelligence for robots.

We do not sell data. We transform it into the map.

The manifold defines what exists. The landscape defines what's optimal. The gradient tells you how to get there.

This is the ontological layer for physical AI.

And it is the most important piece of infrastructure that nobody has built. Until now.
